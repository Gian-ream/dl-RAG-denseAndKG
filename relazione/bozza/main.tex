\begin{abstract}
%
Dense retrieval can surface \emph{distractors} --- passages close to the query but answer-free --- that degrade RAG accuracy, as \citet{Cuconasu_2024} show. We test whether \emph{pure graph topology} on Wikidata can filter them, without using relation semantics. We build a 661.5M-edge entity graph from a local HDT dump, score query--document pairs by topological reachability (connected $\times$ purity), and fuse this with the dense score across a grid of hop-distance, hub-threshold and mixing weight $\alpha$, evaluated on 1000 NQ-open queries with an LLM judge. The result is a clean \emph{negative}: the KG signal never beats the dense baseline (0.364) beyond noise (best 0.372) and actively degrades accuracy as its weight grows (down to 0.290); 13/90 conditions are significant under McNemar+Bonferroni, all of them worse. We explain the mechanism (reachability saturates on a dense graph) and document the engineering and the methodological departures from~\citet{Cuconasu_2024}.
%
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\citet{Cuconasu_2024} show that high-scoring retrieved passages are often \emph{distractors} --- semantically similar to the query yet answer-free --- and degrade the reader. We test one idea against this: filter distractors by \emph{graph proximity} on an open knowledge graph, reranking retrieved passages by how close their entities are, on Wikidata, to the query entities. We deliberately restrict ourselves to pure topology --- bare $\le 3$-hop reachability, no relation semantics --- to isolate what proximity \emph{alone} contributes. The answer is negative: topology alone does not filter distractors. The report's main contribution is this controlled test and the mechanistic account of \emph{why} it fails; a by-product is a method to decide $\le 3$-hop reachability over the entire Wikidata graph without materialising hub neighbourhoods. Code\,\{4\}.

\section{Related work}

We adopt the corpus, retriever and reader of~\citet{Cuconasu_2024}, who characterise the distractor problem in RAG. Retrieval uses Contriever over the gold-augmented Wikipedia corpus of~\citet{Cuconasu_2024}; entity linking uses the ReFinED linker of~\citet{ayoola2022refinedefficientzeroshotcapableapproach}, the best-performing system in the independent evaluation of~\citet{bast2023fairindepthevaluationexisting}. Knowledge-graph approaches to RAG use the graph's relations and semantics; we instead use only its topology. The graph is stored as HDT over a Wikidata dump.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Baseline: dense RAG}
We reproduce the dense-retrieval pipeline of~\citet{Cuconasu_2024}. The corpus is its gold-augmented Dec-2018 Wikipedia release\,\{1\}; we re-segment it on sentence boundaries and pad each passage to exactly 100 words --- the raw DPR passages have ragged, subject-less tails that hurt entity linking and the reader, and since Contriever mean-pools, fixed length is only a consistency choice. Passages are Contriever-encoded and indexed with FAISS (\texttt{IndexFlatIP}, $\sim$42M). From NQ-open\,\{2\} we keep short ($\le 5$-token) answers --- matching the extractive setup of~\citet{Cuconasu_2024} --- whose question and \emph{all} answer variants link to Wikidata entities (ReFinED); we evaluate on a curated 1000-query subset. The top-5 passages feed Llama-2-7B (base) under a one-shot extraction prompt, and correctness is decided by an \emph{LLM judge} (Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, a different model to avoid self-preference) that checks whether the response contains an NQ gold answer --- replacing the brittle substring exact-match of~\citet{Cuconasu_2024} (prompt and judge in the Appendix).

\subsection{Contribution: KG concept-proximity reranking}
We rerank the top-100 by graph proximity on Wikidata. We query a pre-built HDT dump\,\{3\} (Jan 2022, $\sim$166\,GB) via \texttt{pyHDT} --- the public SPARQL endpoint timed out even on a single hub count (see Appendix) --- and export every direct entity-to-entity statement (Wikidata's ``truthy'' \texttt{wdt:} predicates), 661.5M edges. For a pair $(q,d)$ we test $\mathrm{dist}(q,d)\le 3$ \emph{meet-in-the-middle}: distance~1 a set lookup, distance~2 an intersection of 1-hop neighbourhoods $N_1$, distance~3 an indexed edge-probe between $N_1(q)$ and $N_1(d)$ --- never materialising a hub's neighbour list. A degree threshold $t$ drops high-degree bridges. The KG score is $s_{\mathrm{kg}}=\mathrm{cr}\cdot\mathrm{pr}$, where the \emph{connected ratio} $\mathrm{cr}$ is the fraction of query entities reaching $\ge1$ document entity within $k$ hops and the \emph{purity ratio} $\mathrm{pr}$ the fraction of document entities reached by any query entity. We fuse with the dense score,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fuse}
s = (1-\alpha)\,s_{\mathrm{dense}} + \alpha\,s_{\mathrm{kg}},
\end{equation}
with $\alpha$ the \emph{KG weight} and $s_{\mathrm{dense}}=\sigma/\max_q\sigma$ a per-query max-only rescaling of the raw Contriever score $\sigma$ (the Appendix explains why not min--max). We sweep $k\in\{1,2,3\}$, $t\in\{500,1\text{k},2\text{k},5\text{k},10\text{k},\infty\}$, $\alpha\in\{0.1,0.3,0.5,0.7,0.9\}$: 90 rerank cells plus the dense baseline.

\paragraph*{No gain, then harm.} The best cell ($\alpha{=}0.3$, dist~2, $t{=}5\text{k}$) reaches $0.372$: $+0.8$pp $\approx 8$ queries, i.e.\ noise (Table~\ref{tab:results}). As $\alpha$ grows the KG signal dominates and accuracy falls monotonically, bottoming at $0.290$ ($\alpha{=}0.9$, dist~3, $t{=}5\text{k}$).

\paragraph*{It reshuffles, but does not help.} The rerank is far from inert: at $\alpha{=}0.5$ it rewrites the top-5 for $84$--$99.5\%$ of queries depending on the cell (mean Jaccard@5 $0.11$--$0.30$ vs.\ pure retrieval --- typically barely one of five passages survives), yet accuracy stays at the baseline. It reorders aggressively without adding signal.

\paragraph*{Threshold gradient is the mechanism.} Holding $\alpha{=}0.9$, dist~2, accuracy decays monotonically as the hub filter loosens, from $0.354$ ($t{=}500$) to $0.295$ ($t{=}\infty$): admitting high-degree nodes as bridges connects everything to everything and destroys the signal.

\paragraph*{Significance.} Exact McNemar tests against the baseline, Bonferroni-corrected for the 90 comparisons (significance threshold $0.05/90\approx5.6\times10^{-4}$), flag 13 of the 90 conditions as significant --- \emph{all} worse, none better; each flips 170--224 of the 1000 answers yet nets negative.

\section{Discussion and conclusions}

\paragraph*{Why it fails.} On a graph as dense as Wikidata, $2$--$3$-hop reachability \emph{saturates}: almost every document entity is reachable from almost every query entity, so $\mathrm{cr}\approx 1$, the score collapses onto $\mathrm{pr}$ alone, and discrimination is lost; the hub filter limits the damage but recovers no signal. For about 17\% of queries the question entity is itself a very high-degree node (a country, a religion), where any passage is graph-close and the KG score is uninformative.

\paragraph*{Limitations and future work.} Our metric collapses relation semantics (\texttt{P31} \emph{instance-of} and \texttt{P19} \emph{place-of-birth} count alike), so it cannot screen out semantically irrelevant links. The honest next step is to use the graph's relations and semantics --- not just its bare topology.

\paragraph*{Conclusion.} Pure topological proximity does not filter distractors for dense RAG on NQ-open; the value is the explained negative result and the reusable Wikidata tooling.

\section*{Appendix}

\subsection*{Pipeline: notebook flow}
The project is organised as nine notebooks, numbered in execution order. Each notebook reads the artifacts written by the previous ones under \texttt{data/} and writes its own, so every stage is independently resumable and inspectable. The Wikidata graph itself is built separately by the scripts in \texttt{scripts/pipeline/} (Layers~1--3), which run under WSL2 and produce the edge list, the per-node degree statistics and the precomputed 1-hop neighbourhoods that notebook \texttt{07} consumes.
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep 0pt \parskip 0pt
\item \texttt{01\_corpus\_preparation} --- downloads the corpus and segments every article into sentence-aligned, 100-word passages, producing the $\sim$42M-passage retrieval corpus.
\item \texttt{02\_nq\_filtering} --- filters NQ-open down to the queries whose answers are short ($\le$5 tokens) and whose question and answers all carry ReFinED-linkable entities; 31{,}372 queries survive.
\item \texttt{03\_embedding} --- encodes every passage with Contriever and builds nine FAISS shards (the full index does not fit in memory as a single block).
\item \texttt{04\_answer\_preparation} --- runs top-100 dense retrieval for the 1000-query subset and entity-links every retrieved passage.
\item \texttt{05\_answer\_curation} --- identifies the 344 queries whose entire top-100 carries no linkable entities and selects replacement queries from a candidate pool.
\item \texttt{06\_apply\_curation} --- applies the substitution, producing the \texttt{*\_curated} files used by everything downstream.
\item \texttt{07\_kg\_rerank} --- computes KG reachability ($\mathrm{cr}/\mathrm{pr}$) for every query--passage pair over the full $k \times t$ grid, plus the Jaccard reshuffle diagnostics.
\item \texttt{08\_llm\_eval} --- has Llama-2-7B generate an answer for each query under all 91 conditions (one baseline + 90 rerank cells).
\item \texttt{09\_llm\_judge} --- has Qwen2.5 judge every answer against the NQ gold answers and aggregates accuracy and the McNemar tests.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Environment}
Exact reproduction requires \emph{two} environments, because no single platform runs the whole pipeline. The requirements are:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep 0pt \parskip 0pt
\item \textbf{Windows + \texttt{uv}.} The main environment: every notebook runs here, with dependencies pinned in \texttt{pyproject.toml} and installed via \texttt{uv sync}.
\item \textbf{WSL2/Ubuntu for the graph build.} \texttt{pyHDT} requires a C++ build that does not compile cleanly on native Windows, so the three HDT scripts (edge export, label lookup, completeness check) run in a Linux environment that reads the 166\,GB dump through \texttt{/mnt/c}.
\item \textbf{\texttt{faiss-gpu} via \texttt{conda}.} There is no Windows pip wheel for the GPU build of FAISS, so it lives in a separate miniconda installation and is bridged into the \texttt{uv} venv at runtime (\texttt{add\_dll\_directory} for the DLLs plus a \texttt{sys.path} insert for the package); the conda paths are machine-specific and must be adapted in notebook \texttt{04}.
\item \textbf{ReFinED patch.} ReFinED~V1 breaks on Windows + Python~3.12 + transformers~4.48 through three distinct bugs: a Unix-only \texttt{strftime("\%s")} call in its model downloader, \texttt{add\_special\_tokens} passed as a tokenizer-loading kwarg (an error on recent transformers), and regex patterns written without raw strings (a Python~3.12 warning). \texttt{scripts/tooling/patch\_refined.py} patches the installed package in place and is invoked automatically by the notebooks that load the model; it must be re-run after every \texttt{uv sync}.
\item \textbf{HuggingFace token.} Llama-2 is licence-gated, so a personal token must be placed in \texttt{.hf\_token} at the repo root (gitignored); the notebooks read it from there.
\end{itemize}
All numbers reported in this document are recomputed from the raw per-query outputs by \texttt{documents/verify\_report\_numbers.py}, and the schema of every data artifact is catalogued in \texttt{documents/DATA\_DICTIONARY.md}.

\subsection*{Design choices}
\textbf{Dense normalization: max-only, not min--max.} The raw Contriever score $\sigma$ is an inner product over embeddings that are not L2-normalised, so within a query its top-100 values sit in a narrow band (roughly $[0.5,1]$ after rescaling). Before fusing it with the KG score we rescale it by the per-query maximum only, $s_{\mathrm{dense}}=\sigma/\max_q\sigma$. The obvious alternative, min--max normalisation, would stretch that narrow band to the full $[0,1]$ interval --- and in doing so it would change the \emph{philosophy} of the ranking, not just its scale. A concrete example makes the point: take passages A$(\sigma{=}1.05,\,s_{\mathrm{kg}}{=}0.2)$ and B$(\sigma{=}1.00,\,s_{\mathrm{kg}}{=}0.4)$ at $\alpha{=}0.5$. Under max-only scaling, A's dense advantage is what it really is --- about $5\%$ --- so the much larger KG gap dominates and B wins. Under min--max, that same $5\%$ gap can be inflated up to the whole $[0,1]$ range, and the ranking flips to A on the strength of a dense difference that is in fact negligible. Max-only preserves the relative geometry of the dense scores and merely caps them at~1, which is what the fusion in Eq.~\eqref{eq:fuse} assumes.

\textbf{Reader prompt.} We use Llama-2-7B \emph{base}, not the chat variant, so the model is a pure next-token completer with no instruction tuning: prompted naively, it continues ``Answer:'' with Wikipedia-style prose rather than a short extracted span. We therefore prepend a one-shot extraction example that demonstrates both the task and the output format. We also place the question \emph{above} the documents, where \citet{Cuconasu_2024} place it after them: the model reads the question first and can attend back to it while scanning the passages. Following the paper's own finding on position bias, the top-ranked passage is placed last in the context, immediately above the ``Answer:'' slot where the model attends most.

\textbf{Judge.} Substring exact-match --- the metric used by \citet{Cuconasu_2024} --- misses answers that are correct but paraphrased, reformatted, or wrapped in a preamble. We therefore score with an LLM judge that answers YES/NO to ``does the response contain one of the gold answers?''. The judge must be a \emph{different} model from the generator to avoid self-preference bias, so we use Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (Apache-2.0, ungated): strong enough to absorb paraphrase and formatting, cheap enough to judge all 91{,}000 responses locally.

\subsection*{What we tried first}
The reachability machinery described in the report is the result of a sequence of failed attempts; each predecessor failed for an instructive reason.

\textbf{Local triple store $\to$ SPARQL $\to$ local HDT.} Our first attempt at graph access was to download Wikidata and host it locally in a Java triple store (Blazegraph), so we could navigate it without network limits. Ingestion never completed: the working space needed to build the indices blew past the 5\,TB of disk we had available. We fell back to the public Wikidata SPARQL endpoint, which collapsed immediately on popular entities: even a minimal \texttt{COUNT} over a single hub (\texttt{?x wdt:P31 wd:Q5}, ``instances of \emph{human}'') exceeded the server's 60-second limit, and with it fell the whole fallback scheme we had designed around it. We finally switched to a pre-built HDT dump of Wikidata queried locally with \texttt{pyHDT}, where the same predicate-restricted counts go straight to a precomputed index and return in well under a millisecond, with no rate limits.

\textbf{Wildcard $\to$ per-predicate export.} Our first edge exporter walked the whole dump with a wildcard triple iterator and filtered entity-to-entity statements in the loop. On a dump this size the iterator misbehaved in two ways at once: it \emph{over}-iterated (yielding far more triples than the file header declares) while \emph{silently dropping} genuine Q--Q edges --- an inflated traversal with missing data and no error raised. Restricting the iteration to one predicate at a time turned out to be exact: per-predicate counts match the HDT header, the run is deterministic and verifiable, and it recovered $\sim$471k edges the wildcard run had lost, for a final count of $661{,}471{,}158$.

\textbf{BFS-N3 $\to$ meet-in-the-middle.} The proposal's natural reading --- precompute the full 3-hop neighbourhood of every seed by BFS --- timed out on 89\% of seeds in a dry run, including perfectly ordinary, low-degree entities. The diagnosis is the cumulative wave explosion: even when the seed itself is small, its second wave reaches tens of thousands of nodes and the third has to iterate millions of triples. The fix came from noticing that the metric never needs the full neighbourhood, only the \emph{boolean} question ``is this document entity within 3 hops of this query entity?''. That question can be answered by precomputing 1-hop neighbourhoods alone ($\sim$93M rows) and meeting in the middle: distance~1 is a set lookup, distance~2 a set intersection, distance~3 a single indexed edge-probe between the two 1-hop sets. A hub's neighbour list is never enumerated --- we only test membership in it.

\textbf{Query curation.} Once retrieval was in place we found that 344 of the 1000 subset queries had a top-100 with no linkable entities at all, leaving the KG score undefined exactly where it was supposed to act. We replaced those queries with entity-bearing ones drawn from a larger candidate pool. Note the direction of the bias this introduces: the evaluation set is tilted \emph{in favour} of the KG signal, so the negative result should be read as ``even on KG-favourable queries, topological proximity does not help''.
